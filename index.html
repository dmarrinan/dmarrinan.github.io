ABOUT ME

I started programming in high school when I took classes in C++ and Java independent study. I enjoyed these courses, but it was the summer before my freshman year of college that I found a passion for using code to perform modeling and data analysis. I worked for a physicist at the Air Force Research Laboratory analyzing combustion data in MATLAB. Pulling out meaningful information from our data required performing Gaussian fits and extracting statistical information on tens of thousands of time points per experiment. I wrote a graphical user interface in MATLAB to create a dashboard to visualize our data and ensure that we were using the correct fitting parameters for our calculations. This allowed my boss to explore and visualize individual timepoints within of our dataset quickly and efficiently. Instead of wasting hours analyzing our data in one big chunk, he could ensure that our fitting parameters would provide consistent, meaningful results in just a few minutes. This process required that I learn all the stages of data analysis from data storage, retrieval, exploration, transformation, and visualization. I returned to this position for the next two summers as well, and it was at AFRL that I discovered a love of using data analysis and visualization to tell a story; in this case it was about the combustion taking place inside of experimental jet engines.

While at Ohio State I majored in Engineering Physics and learned how to solve challenging, multidisciplinary problems. I was part of the Fundamentals for Engineering Honors program as a freshman. After a quarter of programming in C, C++ and MATLAB this class ended with a quarter long design build project. We built our own robots that used microprocessors and electronic sensors to navigate obstacles and complete challenges. My team won "Best Engineered" out of approximately 60 other teams in large part because of the robust and repeatable performance of my code. I was then selected to be an undergraduate TA for this program for my sophmore through senior years. This really cemented my understanding of basic programming concepts and algorithms and allowed me to master the fundamentals by them teaching others. I also completed an honors undergraduate thesis while at Ohio State that once again required me to perform data analysis and visualization in MATLAB for more combustion experiments.

After graduation I worked for Booz Allen Hamilton performing digital signal processing for radar simulations using Fortran 77. My background in math and science from undergrad allowed me to develop radar models despite the fact that I had taken no electrical engineering classes while at Ohio State. I was then a doctoral candidate in mechanical engineering at the University of Texas at Austin for one year. My past experience at AFRL and Ohio State had led me to believe that I wanted to be an experimentalist. However, while at UT I learned a very important lesson: my passion lies in visualizing data and extracting insights from it, not from designing the scientific experiments to obtain it in the first place!

It was this realization that led me to enroll in the University of Texas at Austin Data Analytics Bootcamp. This program has helped me translate my past experience to technologies that are more relevant for business intelligence including Python/Pandas, SQL, SQLAlchemy, MongoDB, HTML/CSS, Javascript and D3. I enjoy leveraging my mathematical and problem solving skills to utilize these tools to their full potential. I believe that I would be a tremendous resource to any company looking for a candidate to extract new features and trends in their data and to use meaningful data visualizations to communicate these features to clients and stakeholders.

While not doing data analysi you can usually find me caching and indie movie at Alamo Drafthouse, at a concert in the red river district, or out on my bike.
